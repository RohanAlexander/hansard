Ministers, officials, businessmen and senior executives from North America and Europe attended to discuss world economic circumstances and, particularly, trade and payments. The outcome of that meeting was: There was a real need, if we were to try to redress the inflation, unemployment and growth problems, for trade to be liberalised. The purpose of the Live-stock Slaughter Levy Amendment Bill is to amend the Live-stock Slaughter Levy Act 1964 to increase the maximum rates of levy which may be prescribed to finance the Australian Meat and Livestock Corporation, meat and livestock research and cattle disease eradication. The two Acts to be amended today raise funds from the meat and livestock industry for the same purposes and are similar in structure and rates but one applies to livestock slaughtered and the other to stock exported live.
The proposed increases covered by the Bills were recommended by the Australian Meat and Livestock Corporation after consultation with the industry groups. The Government has accepted that the Corporation will need an increase in levy income in order to avoid operating at a deficit or cutting back on its activities and service to the industry and that this will first necessitate an increase in the maximum rates currently provided in the legislation. The levy is imposed on slaughterings of cattle, calves, bobby calves, sheep, lambs, buffaloes and goats. It has separate components for the financing of the Australian Meat and Livestock Corporation, meat and livestock research, meat processing research and cattle disease eradication. There are comparable rates for each component in respect of each class of livestock. The Act specifies the maximum rates only, with operating rates being prescribed by regulation. Separate maximum rates already exist in the legislation for the research and disease eradication components. The Bills propose to double the aggregate maximum rates which govern the size of the AMLC component. For example, for cattle and buffaloes the rate goes from $1.50 to $3 a head.

I may duplicate what has already been said but I think I need to get it into Hansard to let everybody know exactly what the legislation is about. The Minister for the Capital Territory and Minister Assisting the Minister for Industry and Commerce (Mr Hodgman) who is at the table understands.
The decision to adopt this form of assistance follows the Government's acceptance of a recommendation by the Industries Assistance Commission in its report of 24 August 1981 entitled 'Fruit and Fruit Products'. The report covered a wide range of miscellaneous fruit products, and fresh and processed passion fruit and berry fruits.

The Government's decision on that report was announced on 21 April 1982. The Government accepted the lAC view that assistance in the form of a bounty rather than a tariff would be more appropriate in this case. A tariff on imports would have the effect of increasing the cost of locally produced jam and juice products using imported berry fruit and would reduce the competitiveness of those jams and juices againt imports. Furthermore, a tariff was considered not to be an efficient means of assisting producers of berry fruits.
The Bill provides for the bounty to be payable at the rate of $100 per tonne of commercially cultivated fresh fruit provided to the processors of the fruit. The bounty is to be paid directly to the growers of the fruit, and bounty payments will be based on information provided in regular returns to be furnished by the processors. If we are to compete with New Zealand and provide the Australian market with fresh fruit such as strawberries and raspberries we need to be competitive and improve our varieties. It takes into consideration all the variations that occur, such as production, packaging, salesmanship and getting it on to the market. It is a continuous study. The honourable member for Werriwa will agree that it is the same with any other fruit we produce. As I said to the House on 17 November 1981:
Every person is presumed to know the law and this indeed is his duty. Such an onerous duty must surely carry with it the right of unimpeded access to primary sources of the laws.
I am not alone in this view. The Committee on Copyright and Law in the United Kingdom, the Whitford Committee, observed:
The statutes and judgements, embodying as they do the law, should, on the face of it, be free of any possible copyright restrictions.
Sir Laurence Street, the Chief Justice of New South Wales, in his judgment in the case of Greciun-King on I October 1981, said:
In a free and democratic society the law and all its documentation, both statutory and interpretive, that is to say both in Acts of parliament and in judgements, must be 'publici juris'-available to all to be studied, to be used and to be quoted as a matter of public entitlement.
Yet this is not the position in Australia. In the United States of America all laws-that is, all statutes, regulations, cases and tribunal decisions -are in the public domain and can be freely reproduced by anybody. This has not resulted, as is suggested by certain bureaucrats, in a multitude of series of case reports of dubious accuracy. Instead of chaos, the United States legal profession and public are served by very comprehensive, accurate and up to date services ranging from traditional textbooks and case reports to specialised loose-leaf services, digests, indexes and computer data bases. In Australia, however, the Commonwealth and State governments claim that they have a Crown prerogative to publish exclusively cases, statutes and regulations. That this doctrine, with its roots dating back to Tudor times, can still be applied today to restrict basic civil rights in a modern democracy is incredible.
Even though deregulation of Crown copyright in statutes, regulations and cases was advocated by the razor gang report, nothing has yet been done in Australia. What possible arguments are there for retaining Crown copyright? It appears that there are three: Firstly, to ensure accuracy in documents purporting to the cases, statutes or regulations; secondly, to protect the financial

interests of government printers and the publishers of official case reports; and thirdly, to stop a proliferation of case reports. In Australia it appears that these arguments imply government controlled monopolies and almost total prohibition on other publications. In the United States market forces ensure accuracy because sales will fall off if a publication is persistently innaccurate.
In the United Kingdom the Government freely licenses its Crown copyright and will only retract its licence if there are abuses-presumably persistent inaccuracy. In the United States, government printers and publishers of official reports are not protected, they compete. In Britain the Government Printer and the official reports are protected by rules of court requiring that the official copy printed by the Government Printer of a statute or the official report of a case be quoted in court, if it is available, in precedence to other series. In neither Britain nor the United States are there restrictions on specialist series of case reports that deal with cases on a specific subject such as tax, corporate law, criminal law, family law, credit legislation and so on. These series often report a few cases from each jurisdiction and often report cases that are not considered worth reporting in the official reports. It cannot be sensibly argued that a specialist series of case reports is, ipso facto, undesirable, or that it is likely to affect in any way the official reports.
I urge, once again, the Attorney-General (Senator Durack) and the Government to make known the Government's policy on the deregulation of Crown copyright in statutes, cases and regulations and to throw open the Government's copyright laws in an unfettered way, as has been done in America, or else to grant licences freely to all parties seeking them, as is done in Britain. I also urge the Attorney-General to take all steps necessary to ensure that no State hinders the publication of any case of Federal jurisdiction or of national interest.

Mr BRAITHWAITE (Dawson) (10.14)-1 want to make some comments tonight in connection with small business, first of all, specifically to acknowledge what this Government has done within this year's Budget and previously to assist small business. It has increased the retention allowance for trading or business income to 80 per cent. When this Government came to office it was 50 per cent. It has also released from secondary taxation the first $1,000 of company dividends. I believe these measures have been very welcome. Small businesses outside the private company area have been allowed to self-superannuate and claim a deduction at the standard rate of the personal taxation, which rate has been reduced from 32c to 30c in the dollar. Employment schemes directed to training of the unemployed youth and the investment allowance too have been very welcome. I am sure they will be of great assistance.
In a very truncated speech in the grievance debate last Thursday I mentioned one of the difficulties in an area which is very vital to small business, that is, the tourist industry, and the effect of the elimination of the diesel excise rebate on the cost structure of some companies. I want to go on to mention two other difficulties that have been a set-back for the tourist industry, particularly the tourist vessel industry. Firstly, the 40 per cent investment allowance was retrospectively allowed for tourist buses but not vessels. Secondly, a 17-1per cent sales tax was imposed last year on new vessels used in the tourist trade. The Treasury said at that time that that was to correct an anomaly but all it did was create a further and larger anomaly for tourist vessel operators when compared with other operators in the tourist industry. These tourist vessel owners have certainly copped the brunt of the three measures which I have mentioned. Those who had vessels already under construction when the sales tax was applied, became liable for the tax because it was, in fact, retrospectively applied. On the one hand the investment allowance benefits were retrospectively denied and, on the other hand, the sales tax imposition was retrospectively applied. Both measures have penalised the industry.
If the Department of Industry and Commerce is to do its job properly it should move immediately to give relief to all small business enterprises

Mrs CHILD (Henty) (10.19)-ln the Age this morning the editorial was headed 'Merging colleges win a Federal reprieve'. I was interested to learn from it that the Federal Government has now decided to fund the Institute of Early Childhood Development up until the end of the year. The Institute is one of those colleges that the razor gang decided had to amalgamate. It never did tell us why. I have spoken on this topic in the House on three or four previous occasions. I make no apology for speaking on it again. I once again urge the Minister for Education (Senator Peter Baume) to think very carefully before he goes ahead with this plan. He has given a few more months reprieve in which he can rethink the whole idea and leave the Institute of Early Childhood Development where it is. I believe the whole future of the early childhood development field in Australia is at stake when an institute such as the Institute of Early Childhood Development, the only one of its kind in Victoria or, for that matter, in Australia, is asked to amalgamate with some other institute. The proposal is totally irrational. The amalgamations of other colleges have not been economically beneficial, yet the Federal Government maintains that to change the nation's only specialist institute that deals exclusively and effectively with the educational care and development of young children and to blend it with some other college of advanced education will be of some financial gain. We have never had any figures to prove this. We have never had a costing. We are just expected to accept the Minister's word.
More importantly, the research, community programs and training courses offered by the Institute will necessarily deteriorate as a result of the amalgamation. I am genuinely concerned that both the training and the services for Australian children will suffer as a result of the Government's threat both to education and to child care specifically. Australia should be willing to learn from the experience of overseas amalgamations. The warning we got from the disappearance of specialist early childhood colleges and institutes in Great Britain, due to amalgamations and cutbacks, should be heeded by this Government. They have resulted in Britain in a loss of quality in
